# Chapter 10: Sound Waves

## 10.1 Characteristics of Sound Waves

### Introduction

Our surroundings are filled with a variety of sounds. Sound is a form of energy caused by vibration. For example, musical instruments produce sound through vibration. Touch the front of your throat with your fingertips while speaking — you can feel the vibration of your vocal cords.

### Important Concepts

* **Sound requires a medium to propagate.** Sound cannot propagate through a vacuum (an area without air) — this is shown when an alarm clock's ringing cannot be heard after air is sucked out of a bell jar using a vacuum pump.
* **Sound propagates at different speeds in different media:**
  * Sound propagates fastest in solids — particles of solids are arranged very closely together, so vibration in one particle causes nearby particles to also vibrate quickly.
  * Sound propagates less rapidly in liquids compared to solids — due to the looser arrangement of particles in liquid.
  * Sound propagates most slowly in gases — the arrangement of gas particles, which are far apart from each other, causes a delay in the transfer of vibration.
* **Transfer of sound:** When a bell is rung, the metal surface of the bell vibrates. Air molecules near the surface also vibrate and collide with nearby air molecules. This vibration is transferred from one molecule to another in the form of waves, until the sound can be heard by the listener's ears.
* **Reflection and Absorption of Sound:** Sound can be reflected and absorbed when it hits the surface of an object — these characteristics are the same as the characteristics of light learned in Form One. The amount of sound reflected or absorbed depends on the surface of the object.
  * Hard and smooth surfaces are good sound reflectors (e.g. marble tiles, walls).
  * Soft and rough surfaces are good sound absorbers (e.g. carpet, softboard).

### Keywords

* Vibration
* Medium
* Wave
* Amplitude
* Frequency
* Reflection of sound
* Absorption of sound

### Important Facts

* Activity 10.1 shows the sound of an alarm clock cannot be heard after air is sucked out of a bell jar (proving sound needs a medium).
* Different loudness levels can be felt by placing an ear on plastic containers filled with air, water and flour separately.
* An activity comparing surfaces (wooden plank, metal sheet, towel) shows that a metal surface (hard and smooth) reflects sound well, while a towel (soft and rough) absorbs sound well.

### Examples

* The sound of aircraft engines can be heard because sound can propagate through air.
* The walls of cinema halls are usually covered with thin softboards to absorb sound and reduce echo.

### Subtopic Summary

Sound is energy caused by vibration and requires a medium (solid, liquid or gas) to propagate — it cannot propagate through a vacuum. The speed of sound propagation differs by medium: fastest in solids, followed by liquids, and slowest in gases, based on the closeness of particle arrangement. Sound can also be reflected by hard, smooth surfaces, or absorbed by soft, rough surfaces.

## 10.2 Loudness and Pitch of Sound

### Introduction

Our ears can differentiate the sounds that are heard because sounds have different strengths and pitches. The strength or loudness of sound depends on the amplitude of the sound wave, while the pitch of sound depends on the frequency of the sound produced. Frequency is measured in hertz (Hz).

### Important Concepts

* **Loudness of sound** depends on the **amplitude** of the sound wave — the greater the amplitude, the louder the sound produced.
* **Pitch of sound** depends on the **frequency** of the sound wave — the higher the frequency, the higher the pitch of sound produced.
* Activity 10.2 uses a Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (C.R.O.) and an audio signal generator: increasing output power increases the amplitude (louder sound); increasing frequency increases the wave frequency (higher pitch of sound).
* **Doppler Effect:** The apparent change in frequency caused by the relative movement of the sound source, the relative movement of the observer, or both.
  * The frequency of an ambulance siren heard by an observer increases when the ambulance approaches the stationary observer.
  * The frequency of the ambulance siren heard by the observer decreases when the ambulance drives away from the stationary observer.

### Keywords

* Loudness
* Pitch
* Amplitude
* Frequency
* Hertz (Hz)
* Doppler effect

### Important Facts

* The mooing of a cow is a low frequency sound; the squeaking of a rat is a high frequency sound.
* The way we press piano keys determines the strength of the sound produced.
* Activity 10.4 uses an air horn to study the Doppler effect — the pitch of sound heard by an observer changes while the air horn passes by, but the person carrying the sound source does not feel this change in pitch.

### Examples

* The squeak of a rat compared to the moo of a cow shows the difference in frequency between different animals.
* The Doppler effect can be observed when an emergency vehicle (ambulance) passes by — the pitch of the siren changes as it approaches and moves away from an observer.

### Subtopic Summary

The loudness of sound is determined by the amplitude of the sound wave, while the pitch of sound is determined by the frequency (measured in Hz). The Doppler effect explains the apparent change in sound frequency caused by relative movement between the sound source and the observer — for example, the change in pitch of an ambulance siren as it approaches and moves away from a person.

## 10.3 Phenomena and Application of Reflection of Sound Waves

### Introduction

Reflection of sound waves is one of the characteristics of sound waves learned in 10.1. This characteristic produces a phenomenon known as echo. Apart from that, reflection of sound waves is also used in various devices and sectors.

### Important Concepts

* **Echo:** Produced when sound waves are reflected from a hard surface to the listener. The reflected echo sounds the same as the original sound but takes some time to reach the listener's ears. An echo can be heard in places like enclosed halls, empty rooms, caves, tunnels and gorges.
* **Ultrasound:** A type of sound wave with a frequency of more than 20,000 Hz. Ultrasound cannot be heard by humans but can be heard by animals such as bats that use it for navigation.
* **Sonar:** A sound reflection technology used in the shipping industry to detect underwater objects. This technology is also used in other sectors, such as medical and fisheries.
* **Limitations of Hearing:** The frequency of sound that can be detected by humans is within the range of 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. This range becomes narrower as we age because our ears lose their sensitivity to sound frequencies. Animals such as bats, dolphins and dogs have a higher range of hearing than humans.

### Keywords

* Echo
* Ultrasound
* Sonar
* Limit of hearing
* Hertz (Hz)

### Important Facts

* **Table: Hearing range of animals (Hz):** Bats 2,000–110,000; Dolphins 40–100,000; Dogs 67–45,000; Elephants 16–12,000; Horses 55–33,500.
* Devices to overcome human limitations of hearing: stethoscopes (help doctors listen to a patient's heartbeat), megaphones (amplify the voice so it can be heard from a distance), hearing aids (amplify sound entering the ear).
* Repeated echo in a cave is caused by sound being reflected back and forth repeatedly between the hard surfaces of the cave.

### Examples

* Ultrasound waves are used to scan the condition of a foetus in the womb (sonogram).
* Bats use ultrasound for distance estimation and navigation in the dark.
* Recording studios are lined with sound-absorbing materials to reduce echo and produce high-quality sound recordings.

### Subtopic Summary

The reflection of sound waves produces the phenomenon of echo when sound is reflected from a hard surface. Ultrasound (frequency >20,000 Hz) is used in sonar technology for various sectors, including shipping, medical and fisheries. Humans have a limit of hearing (20 Hz–20,000 Hz) that is lower than some animals, and devices such as stethoscopes, megaphones and hearing aids are used to overcome this limitation.
