# Chapter 11: Stars and Galaxies in the Universe

## 11.1 Stars and Galaxies in the Universe

### Introduction

The universe consists of every existing thing around us. There are many objects in space which we may have never seen or known of their existence. The study of astronomy has raised our awareness of the beauty and vastness of God's creation of the universe. Technology plays an important role in the study of outer space — the Hubble Space Telescope was launched on 24th April 1990 and is so powerful that it could see a coin from 725 km away.

### Important Concepts

* **Galaxy:** A set of bodies consisting of millions of stars with gas and dust particles. Galaxies come in many forms:
  * **Spiral galaxies:** examples are Andromeda and the Milky Way.
  * **Elliptical galaxies:** examples are Ursa Major and Messier 87.
  * **Irregular galaxies:** examples are the Small Magellanic Cloud and Large Magellanic Cloud.
* **The Milky Way:** A medium-large spiral galaxy. Our solar system is located at the edge of one of its spiral arms. The Milky Way consists of approximately 200 billion stars, and the Sun is one of them.
* **The Life Cycle of a Star (Nebular Hypothesis):**
  1. Stars are formed from nebulae — large clouds consisting of dust particles and gases such as hydrogen and helium.
  2. The gases and dust particles in a nebula are pulled by a strong gravitational force, causing it to form a globe.
  3. The strong gravitational force causes the globe of gas to shrink and compress until it becomes very dense and forms a core.
  4. When the temperature and pressure in the core become too high, a nuclear reaction takes place — hydrogen gas turns into helium, releasing a huge amount of heat energy and light.
  5. The core shines and a star is formed — this new star is known as a **protostar**.
  6. This new star continues to expand and becomes either an average star like the Sun or a massive star.
  * **Death of Stars:** The outermost layer of a star heats up and hydrogen within it starts to burn, causing the star to expand and appear red — called a **red giant**.
    * If the red giant is not massive, a **white dwarf** is formed.
    * If the red giant is big enough, it contracts quickly so that a big explosion called a **supernova** occurs. If the original star is a large star, a **neutron star** is formed; if the original star is a super-large star, a **black hole** is formed (light cannot escape from it).

### Important Facts

* **Relative size comparison:** Earth < Solar System < The Milky Way < A group of galaxies < A cluster of galaxies < Universe. The Earth is smaller than a speck of dust compared to the universe.
* **Characteristics to classify stars:** colour, temperature, size, brightness, distance (from Earth).
* **Table: Classification of stars based on colour and temperature (K):** Red (<3,500), Orange (3,500–5,000), Yellow (5,000–6,000), Yellowish-white (6,000–7,500), White (7,500–11,000), Bluish-white (11,000–25,000), Blue (>25,000).
* Really big stars are called **supergiant stars**; big stars are called **giant stars**; really small ones are called **dwarf stars**.
* The brightness of a star depends on its size, distance and surface temperature. The brightest stars in the sky are Sirius and Rigel.
* Dato' Dr. Sheikh Muszaphar Shukor was the first Malaysian to venture into outer space, on 10th October 2007. Nur Adlyka Ainul Annuar proved the existence of a supermassive black hole in early 2017.

### Examples

* Andromeda and the Milky Way — examples of spiral galaxies.
* Ursa Major and Messier 87 — examples of elliptical galaxies.
* The Small Magellanic Cloud and Large Magellanic Cloud — examples of irregular galaxies.
* The National Planetarium in Kuala Lumpur, officially opened in 1994, combines Islamic architecture with astronomy.

### Subtopic Summary

The universe contains millions of galaxies, including the Milky Way, which houses our solar system. Stars are formed from nebulae through a process of gravitational collapse and nuclear reaction (birth), and end as a white dwarf, neutron star, or black hole (death), depending on the original size of the star. Stars can be classified based on their colour, temperature, size, brightness and distance from Earth.
